Market Overview

The music product market is segmented into three major submarkets, the sizes of which are presented below. BitLabs is positioning its first product, OrBit, within the rapidly growing music production market. Moreover, integration of the product with music composition software creates value at the intersection between the two expanding markets.

Music Composition Software

The global music composition software market was valued at $79.2 million in 2017 and is forecast to increase at compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 21.4% during 2018 – 2023. (P&S Market Research, 2018)

Music Instrument Retail

The music instrument retail industry revenue is estimated to increase at a CAGR of 2.8% to reach £458.4 million in 2019. (IBIS World, 2018)

Music Production Software

Analysts forecast the global music production software market to grow at a CAGR of 8.65% during the period 2018-2022. (Business Wire, 2018) The projected market size in 2026 is $16.5 billion. (Transparency Market Research, 2018)


Technological advances in the music production industry have led to the emergence of advanced mobile-based software, which is used to mix and compose tracks. The growing preference for live events and electronic music has led to an increase in the number of DJs worldwide and a general shift to digital music within the industry.

BitLabs leverages the growth in both the music composition and production markets, as well as the increased demand for live events. As public interest for electronic music rises, there is a business opportunity for a product that allows more musicians to use digital effects.

User Base

User Segmentation

Our user base is heavily segmented into subgroups that range from beginners to professionals. Moreover, music production does not neccessarily mean playing an instrument, but also includes sound engineers and producers. An initial user base segmentation focused on grouping this fragmented base into nine sugroups as presented below.

User Base Segmentation

Users can be categorised in terms of level of expertise and type of musician

BitLabs chose to narrow down its target market to the performer subgroup early on. OrBit is designed to augment performance through digital elements, but could still be used in a studio setting. Performers were further broken down into the following:

Consumer

Consumers may not have much music experience but have a general interest in music gadgets. They usually have a limited budget for a device and use it primarily for entertainment. The interaction needs to be fun for them in order to keep them interested.

Prosumer

Prosumers have more experience with music products and buy them primarily to expand their musical possiblities. They hope the device can be a tool which improves their music and helps them gain more exposure. They might tend to buy a product which is more of a tool rather than a toy. More importantly, OrBit needs to give them useful feedback and allow them to interact with music in a meaningful way.

Professional

For music professionals or performing artists precise control over their performance is very important. Therefore, their requirements on functionality are higher than any other user group. The device must be robust, reliable and exact.

User Preference Analysis

The three performer subgroups were analysed individually in terms of their requirements and aspirations. While a consumer is looking for more of a toy and is happy with a 'gimmicky' product, a professional user needs exact control and as little distraction as possible. Finally, a prosumer is located somewhere between the two. Prosumers will aspire to own a product that can operate at a professional level, but accept a certain degree of imprecision within the device.

User Preference Analysis

BitLabs has analysed the current market and identified which product characteristics are most important to the performer subgroups.

From primary (shown below) and secondary research, BitLabs has decided to focus on the prosumer market. The favourable ratio between spending power and desired functionality as well as the market size contributed to this decsision. The spending power of consumers would not be aligned with the hardware required for OrBit to work. Conversely, professional musicians require a level of control which would be unrealistic to achieve with a first product. Our business model (refer to Investor section), however, still aims to excite musicians of all levels of expertise through a freely available mobile demo application. In this way, BitLabs aims to speak to all branches of the heavily segmented music market and inspire consumers to expand their musical scope.

The Competition

The paradigm for controlling music software are traditional MIDI controllers. These allow musicians to control digital effects using physical objects such as pads, knobs or sliders. This manual form of input is not compatible with playing an instrument where the hands are occupied. Existing products that attempt to overcome this restriction include motion-sensing rings (Enhancia, Wave, HotHand). Both of them are early-stage startups.

OrBit offers two key differentiators. Firstly, it has a versatile mode of attachment. Our competitors designed their product to only attach to a finger, essentially forcing any instrument player to couple their playing motions to an effect. BitLabs on the other hand gives musician freedom on where they attach the device and how they control effects. Secondly, current motion-sensing products do not offer refined tactile interactions. BitLabs has found a combination of the two to give more control over digital effects. OrBit will combine the freedom of motion control with the practicality of manual controls on the device.

Development Timeline

Concept

The product was conceived with the vision of enabling acoustic musicians to access and control digital effects with motion.

Initial User Engagement

We have engaged students and staff from the Royal College of Music in London from the beginning of the project. This research revealed that there would be a limited demand for our product from classical musicians. This shifted the focus towards electronic and contemporary musicians.

First Prototype

The product was prototyped using the sensors in a smartphone and was trialed with musicians. This process highlighted areas for improvement and key fuctionality that needed to be implemented.

Musician Surveys

Using low-fidelity prototypes, user feedback was collected in order to understand where instrumentalists might like to put the device. This informtion was directly used in the development of the first generation of attachments. Key insights included that almost all interviewees were willing to attach the device to their instrument and that suction cups were an apporprite means of attachment.



Concept Development

Insights from the surveys were used to develop six key methods of attachment including a suction cup, clip and strap.

Model

The clip and strap were combined into one attachment and the suction cup into another in order to be prototyped and tested.

Business Development

Strategy

BitLabs are taking a pragmatic approach to business development. We know that hardware iterations are expensive and that manufacturing costs present a significant barrier to entry. Releasing OrBit as a high-quality, innovative device for musicians remains our target, however, a strong strategy for adding value to the enterprise along the way is essential.

For these reasons we have been developing a smartphone-based version of our product in tandem with the development of OrBit. This mobile app allows users to gain control of music software, enabled through the reading of the motion sensors embedded in all modern smartphones (iOS + Android). This product is released in line with a Freemium strategy in which the user is forced to upgrade to the hardware version to attain significant performance control. The mobile prototype is good enough to excite a user and make them want to perform with the technology, but will lack the attachability and physical interface of the real device. One of our major insights from research was that musicians want to test products before they buy them. The Freemium strategy allows BitLabs to reach a global user base and excite them about what OrBit could do for to their performance.

Revenue Structure

The software and physical device are distributed through different channels

Route to Market

Rather than advertising OrBit as a product, we aim to get our free mobile prototype to as many musicians as possible. This exploits the ease of spreading digital assets and opens up the adressable market to a global market of musicians with a smartphone and Internet access. Of course, the challenge of making the free app known to potential customers remain. BitLabs is following a three-point approach to achieve that.

Social Media

We will use targeted advertising to match viewers with musical aspirations to our adverts.

Featured Artists

BitLabs will exploit its location in London, a musical hotspot, to spotlight local and international musicians using our product.

Community

BitLabs knows that the music scene is highly connected, which is why we aim to provide a community platform. Presets can be distributed and downloaded by users. By making our mobile prototype accessible to both beginners and experienced musicians, we want to share the excitement our product can bring with everyone.

Once an initial base of interested customers has been established, OrBit will be targeted at these users. Musicians are likely to have friends that play music and BitLabs will try to target ads to our user's social network.

Revenue Streams

BitLabs intends to capitalise on OrBit through a direct sales model. The product will be advertised on our website, through our free application, and in selected music stores. The current retail price is estimated between £100 and £200 pounds. We will sell OrBit through multiple channels, including both online stores as well as physical retail spaces.

A secondary revenue stream arises from ad placement on our mobile application. This will create less value, however, is essential to support the business in its early stages.

The final revenue stream are the accessories and digital presets that will be sold through BitLabs's Asset Store. This will include specific attachments mechanisms and complex filter presets for specific musicians (e.g. a reverb preset for vocalists).

IP Strategy

BitLabs has created IP during the development process and is aiming to protect it as part of a rigorous IP protection strategy. The software back-end will only be supplied to the customer in a compiled form, making it impossible to retrieve the original source code.

With regards to physical mechanisms, BitLabs is looking at protecting its versatile and modular attachments. The company is currently in the process of creating an invention disclosure and will be liasing with Imperial Innovations to evaluate whether patent protection is feasible. Specifically, the patent would cover a wireless, modular, motion-sensing device with versatile attachment mechanisms for controlling digital effects.

The Future

BitLabs is designing OrBit using tried and tested tech and linking the product to existing software to keep development costs to a minimum. Our immediate goal is to develop the mobile prototype and platform to an extent where they can be distributed digitally.

Simulatneously, the physical prototype will be developed to a fully functional level and IP protection will be sought. Our subsequent funding campaign will be driven by the aim of miniaturising the tech, manufacturing the product, and making it available to customers through our previously established network.
